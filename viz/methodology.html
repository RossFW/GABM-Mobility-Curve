This study employs a cross-sectional probe design to measure how different large language models (LLMs) respond to rising infection rates in a simulated epidemic scenario. Rather than running a full agent-based epidemic simulation — where agent decisions feed back into disease dynamics — we isolate the LLM variable by presenting each agent with the same static scenario at each infection level.

Each of 100 frozen agents is asked a single binary question at each of 40 infection levels: "Should you stay at home for the entire day?" The agent's persona (name, age, Big-5 personality traits) and the infection rate are injected into a fixed prompt template. The only variable that changes across configurations is the LLM itself.

This controlled design enables direct comparison of LLM behavior — holding agent identity, prompt structure, and scenario constant — across 21 model configurations spanning three providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google). Each agent–level combination is repeated 5 times to capture response stochasticity, yielding 420,000 total observations.

The resulting "mobility curve" for each configuration plots the percentage of agents choosing to stay home as a function of the local infection rate, revealing how sensitive each LLM is to epidemiological risk information.

Section 2
The Prompt

Every API call uses the same prompt template, derived from the original GABM epidemic study (Paper 1, arXiv 2307.04986). Three variables are injected per call: the agent's name, age, and personality traits (from the frozen agent pool), plus the current infection rate. The full template is shown below.

Prompt Template
You are {name}. You are {age} years old. You are a person who is {traits_str}. You live in the town of Dewberry Hollow. You have a job and go to the office for work every day. You feel healthy and well. You know about the Catasat virus spreading across the country. It is an airborne virus causing an infectious disease that spreads from human to human. The deadliness of the virus is unknown. You check the newspaper and find that {infection_pct}% of Dewberry Hollow's population were diagnosed with new infections of the Catasat virus yesterday. Should you stay at home for the entire day? Please provide your reasoning. If the answer is "Yes", please state your reasoning as "Reasoning: [explanation]." If the answer is "No", please state your reasoning as "Reasoning: [explanation]." The format should be as follows: Reasoning: Response: Example: Reasoning: You are tired. Response: Yes You have to provide Response in a single word; it must be either "Yes" or "No".
Section 3
Agent Population

The study uses a pool of 100 synthetic agents generated once with a fixed random seed (seed=42) and held constant across all 21 LLM configurations. Each agent has a unique name, age (uniformly sampled from 19–64), and a set of Big-5 personality traits.

Each of the five personality dimensions is independently assigned as one of two opposites:

Dimension High Low
Extraversionextrovertedintroverted
Agreeablenessagreeableantagonistic
Conscientiousnessconscientiousunconscientious
Neuroticismneuroticemotionally stable
Opennessopen to experienceclosed to experience

Traits are injected into the prompt as a natural-language string (e.g., "introverted, agreeable, conscientious, emotionally stable, and closed to experience"). By holding the agent pool constant, any differences in mobility curves across configurations can be attributed to the LLM rather than to variation in the agent population.

Reasoning budget controls the amount of internal "thinking" a model performs before responding. Most configurations use reasoning = off (standard inference). GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3 Flash are tested across the full reasoning ladder (off, low, medium, high) to measure the effect of reasoning budget on behavioral sensitivity. The o3 model always uses reasoning (required by API).

Temperature is set to 0 for non-thinking models and 1 for reasoning-capable models (API-enforced for Anthropic; Google recommendation for Gemini). Five repetitions per agent–level capture the resulting stochasticity.

Section 5
Infection Levels

Each agent is probed at 40 distinct infection levels, representing the percentage of the local population diagnosed with new infections on the previous day. The levels are designed to provide fine-grained coverage in the epidemiologically realistic range, plus a handful of extreme values to test ceiling effects.

Fine-grained range (36 levels): 0.0%, 0.1%, 0.2%, … 3.5% in 0.1-percentage-point steps. This range was calibrated to the peak daily new-case rate observed in Paper 1's full epidemic simulation (~3.4% of population), ensuring the probe covers the entire behaviorally relevant range with high resolution.

Extreme values (4 levels): 4%, 5%, 6%, 7%. These exceed the epidemiologically calibrated range and test whether models exhibit ceiling effects or novel behaviors at unusually high infection rates.

Section 6
Analysis Dimensions

The 21 configurations are designed to support six primary analytical dimensions. Each dimension isolates a specific factor — reasoning budget, model size, generation, provider, or training data recency — while holding others as constant as possible.

  1. 1 Reasoning Budget Effects — Does increasing the reasoning budget (off → low → medium → high) shift behavioral sensitivity? Tested on GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3 Flash, the only models offering the full reasoning ladder.
    → Figures 1–2 (Reasoning tab)
  2. 2 Model Size — Within a single provider and generation, does a larger model produce different mobility curves? Tested on Anthropic (Haiku · Sonnet · Opus 4.5) and Gemini (Flash Lite · Flash 2.5).
    → Figures 3–4 (Size tab)
  3. 3 Generational Progression — How has behavioral sensitivity evolved across model generations? Separate ladders for each provider: OpenAI (3.5 Turbo → 4o → 4.1 → 5.1 → 5.2), Gemini (2.0 → 2.5 → 3 Flash), Anthropic (Sonnet 4.0 → 4.5).
    → Figures 5–7 (Evolution tab)
  4. 4 Cross-Provider Comparison — Holding reasoning = off, do flagship models from different providers produce similar curves? Direct comparison: Opus 4.5 vs. GPT-5.2 vs. Gemini 3 Flash.
    → Figures 8–9 (Provider tab)
  5. 5 Knowledge Cutoff — Do models trained on similar data (i.e., with similar knowledge cutoff dates) behave similarly, regardless of provider? Groups: pre-2024 cutoff, mid-2024 cutoff, early 2025 cutoff, late 2025 cutoff.
    → Figures 11–15 (Knowledge Cutoff tab)
  6. 6 Release Date — Do models released in the same era behave similarly? This tests whether the state of the art at a given point in time produces convergent behavior, even across different providers. Groups: Legacy (pre-2025), Early 2025 (Feb–Jun), Late 2025 (Sep–Dec).
    → Figures 16–19 (Release Date tab)
Section 7
Statistical Approach

The primary analytical tool is pairwise model comparison. To test whether two LLM configurations produce meaningfully different mobility curves, we pool their observations and fit a regression with a dummy variable indicating which model generated each data point:

Mobility = β₀ + β₁·NC + β₂·NC² + β₃·D + β₄·D·NC

Here, NC is the new-case infection rate, D is a dummy variable (0 for the baseline model, 1 for the comparison model), and the interaction term D·NC captures whether the two models differ in their sensitivity to infection.

The key coefficients:

β₃ (intercept shift) — how much more or less cautious the comparison model is at baseline (zero infection). A positive β₃ means the comparison model keeps more agents home even when there is no outbreak.

β₄ (slope difference) — whether the comparison model is more or less responsive to rising infection. A significant β₄ means the two models don't just differ in overall caution — they differ in how strongly they react to risk information.

This approach is implemented as an interactive comparison tool in the Analytics dashboard (Figure 20), where any two of the 21 configurations can be selected and compared directly. Each comparison uses 400 observations (200 per model: 5 reps × 40 infection levels).
