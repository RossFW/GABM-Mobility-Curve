GABM Mobility Curve

How do different LLMs respond to a simulated epidemic? · Paper 3 · Ross Williams PhD Dissertation
Abstract
[Abstract placeholder — to be written after results are finalized.]

This study probes 21 large language model configurations across three providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google) to measure how LLM-driven agents respond to rising infection rates in a simulated epidemic scenario. Using a cross-sectional design — 100 frozen agents × 40 infection levels × 5 repetitions — we generate "mobility curves" that reveal each model's behavioral sensitivity to epidemiological risk. Dissertation Context

This is the third paper in a PhD dissertation exploring the use of large language models as generative agents in agent-based models (GABMs).

Paper 1 introduced the GABM framework, using GPT-3.5 to drive agent behavior in a full epidemic simulation. Agents responded to infection rates by deciding whether to stay home, producing realistic epidemic curves without hand-coded behavioral rules.

Paper 2 investigated prompt sensitivity — demonstrating that small changes in how agents are prompted can meaningfully shift their behavior, raising questions about the robustness of LLM-driven simulations.

Paper 3 (this study) holds the prompt constant and varies the LLM itself. By testing 21 model configurations across three providers, we ask: does it matter which model you use? The mobility curve — plotting the share of agents who stay home as a function of infection rate — is the primary tool for answering that question.
