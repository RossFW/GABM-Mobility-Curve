Author Note — Statistical Methods
Understanding Spearman’s Rank Correlation (ρ)

1. What is Spearman’s rank correlation?

A non-parametric measure of how well two variables’ rankings agree. Unlike Pearson correlation, it doesn’t assume linearity — it only cares about ordering. If agents who rank high in one model also rank high in another, ρ is close to +1.

  • ρ = +1: perfect agreement (identical rankings)
  • ρ = 0: no relationship (random orderings)
  • ρ = −1: perfect disagreement (reversed rankings)

2. Worked example with 5 agents

Consider 5 agents simulated by two different LLM configs. Each agent’s stay-home rate is the proportion of decisions where they chose to stay home.

AgentConfig A rateRank AConfig B rateRank Bdd²
Alice0.8550.72411
Bob0.3010.452−11
Carol0.6030.55300
Dave0.4520.38111
Eve0.7040.805−11

Step 1: Rank each column from lowest (1) to highest (5).

Step 2: Compute d = Rank A − Rank B for each agent.

Step 3: Apply the formula:

ρ = 1 − (6 × Σd²) / (n × (n² − 1))

ρ = 1 − (6 × 4) / (5 × 24) = 1 − 24/120 = 1 − 0.2 = 0.80

Interpretation: ρ = 0.80 indicates strong agreement — the two models largely agree on which agents are cautious and which are bold.

3. How we use it in Figure 27

  • Instead of 5 agents, we have 100
  • Instead of 2 configs, we compute ρ for all 21 × 21 = 441 pairs (210 unique off-diagonal pairs)
  • Each agent’s stay-home rate is computed across all 200 decisions (40 infection levels × 5 reps)
  • High ρ across model pairs → agents are “real individuals” whose traits drive behavior consistently
  • Low ρ → the LLM model matters more than the agent’s traits

4. Interpreting the heatmap

  • Dark cells = high ρ (strong agreement on agent ordering)
  • Light cells = low ρ (models rank agents differently)
  • The diagonal is always 1.0 (a config correlated with itself)
  • Look for provider blocks: do models from the same provider agree more with each other than with models from other providers?
