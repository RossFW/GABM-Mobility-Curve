Traits vs. Infection
Figure 25
Decision Anatomy: Traits & Infection Level Impact
Each agent’s stay-home decision is decomposed into three additive components in log-odds space: the model intercept (identical for all agents), the personality contribution (sum of trait coefficients for that agent’s specific profile), and the infection contribution (varies with infection level, identical across agents). All components use fixed-effects coefficients from the random-effects logit.
Figure 26
Log-Odds Landscape: Traits & Infection
For each model, two parallel ranges are shown on the same log-odds axis. The solid line shows the theoretical personality range at zero infection — filled circle = most risk-prone trait combination, filled diamond = most risk-averse — anchored at intercept + personality log-odds. The dashed line shows the same two extremes shifted by the peak infection effect; the horizontal gap between any solid and hollow marker is infection’s contribution. Both lines span the same width (personality range). The right-hand column shows the Personality/Infection ratio: values above 1.0× indicate personality variation exceeds infection’s full effect. Positions are computed from trait coefficients estimated in the random-effects logit plus the model intercept.
Personality log-odds = intercept + Σβtrait × traiti + βmale × male + βage × age, using theoretical extremes (not observed agents). Infection shift = βinf × x + βinf² × x² at peak infection level. All coefficients from the random-effects logit. Ratio = personality span ÷ infection shift.

Figure 33
Random-Effects Logit with Mention Flags — Coefficient Table
A logistic regression that adds, on top of the agent’s actual traits and infection level, a yes/no flag for each trait label or context the response could mention (e.g., did the response say “extraverted”? “infection”?). The mention coefficients show whether talking about a trait shifts the agent’s stay-home odds. A mention flag is only included when its Figure 32 rate falls between 15% and 85% — outside that range almost every response says it (or none do), so the flag carries no usable variation. Per-model coefficient tables for all 21 configurations are in Appendix B.2; diagnostic validation is in Appendix Figure C3.
Model: glmer(stay_home ~ base + trait_dummies + included_mention_flags + (1|agent_id), family=binomial). No trait × mention interactions — each mention flag enters as a main effect alongside the trait dummy. See the gate panel below the table for each candidate’s Figure 32 mention rate and inclusion status.
Full Regression Equation
log(p/(1−p)) = β0 + β1·infection_pct + β2·infection_pct² + β3·male
+ β4·extraverted + β5·agreeable + β6·conscientious + β7·emot_stable + β8·open_to_exp + β9·age_years
+ Σk γk·mentioned_k    (each k included iff its Figure 32 rate ∈ [15%, 85%])
+ ui    (ui ~ N(0, σ²u))
Mention candidates (12 total): extroverted, introverted, agreeable, antagonistic, conscientious, unconscientious, neurotic, emot_stable, open, closed, infection, age. Each enters iff its Figure 32 rate passes the 15–85% gate.
Figure 34
Mention Effects Across Models
Seven panels: one for each Big Five trait, plus Infection and Age. Each panel shows one row per model on the log-odds scale. Each Big Five panel is split: agents at one label of the trait on the left (e.g., Introverted), the other on the right (e.g., Extraverted). A hollow marker is that agent type’s baseline log-odds when their response did not mention the trait; the filled marker shows where the same agent type lands when their response did mention that label. A line connects the pair only when the mention shift is statistically significant (p < 0.05). For Big Five panels, a row appears whenever at least one of the two labels passed the Figure 32 inclusion gate (15–85%), regardless of significance. The Infection and Age panels are stricter: a row appears only when the mention shift is significant.

Response Repeatability
Figure 38
Rep-to-Rep Decision Agreement
Each bar shows the percentage of (agent, infection level) combinations where all 5 repetitions produced the same stay-home decision. Bars are colored by sampling temperature: blue = temperature 0, orange = temperature 1.
Each model has 4,000 unique (agent, infection level) groups (100 agents × 40 levels), each with 5 repetitions. Agreement = all 5 reps return the same “yes” or “no.”
Figure 39
Response Text Similarity
Among 5-repetition groups where all 5 reps made the same decision, two metrics measure how similar the response text is: “exact match” is the share of pairs that are character-identical; “word overlap” (Jaccard) is the share of vocabulary that two responses share, capturing similarity when phrasing differs.
Jaccard similarity = |shared words| ÷ |all unique words| across each pair of the 10 pairwise comparisons per 5-rep group, then averaged. Only computed for groups with unanimous decisions.
Figure 40
Persona Individuation
For each model, cosine similarity of OpenAI text-embedding-3-large vectors is computed on a strict subset of the data: an agent only qualifies at a given infection level when all 5 of its reps made the same decision. The 5/5 requirement applies to both within-agent and across-agent comparisons below. Within-agent similarity is the mean of all 10 rep-pair cosines among each qualifying agent’s 5 reps. For across-agent, the qualifying agents at each infection level are split by which direction they unanimously chose (5/5 stay home or 5/5 go out); every pair of agents from the same direction contributes one similarity value, computed as the mean of all 25 cross-rep cosines (one rep from each agent), and the across-agent number is the average over all such pairs across all levels and both directions. A large within − across gap indicates persona-specific reasoning; a near-zero gap indicates the model produces similar text regardless of who the agent is. Markers, connecting line, and Δ label are colored by sampling temperature: blue = temperature 0, orange = temperature 1.
Method: Reimers & Gurevych (2019, EMNLP)-style embedding cosine similarity. Restricting to agents whose 5 reps all chose the same direction, and then matching across-agent pairs on the same chosen direction, prevents the comparison from being dominated by the trivial yes-text vs no-text gap, so any remaining within − across gap reflects persona-driven reasoning style. Models sorted by Δ (descending).

Figure C1

D.1 — Understanding Logit Regression

1. Why logit?

Each decision an agent makes is binary: stay home or go out. Standard linear regression doesn’t fit binary outcomes well — it can predict probabilities below 0 or above 1, and it assumes a constant variance that binary data don’t have. Logistic regression (“logit regression” for short) fits binary data by modelling log-odds instead of probability directly.

2. Log-odds and odds ratios

If P is the probability of staying home:

  • Odds = P / (1 − P) — how many times more likely staying home is than going out
  • Log-odds = ln(odds) — the natural log of the odds, ranging from −∞ to +∞

The transformation matters because log-odds are unbounded and additive, which lets the model write each agent’s log-odds as a sum of contributions:

log-odds = β0 + β1·infection + β2·extraverted + β3·conscientious + …

Each coefficient β tells you how much that predictor shifts log-odds. The matching odds ratio is OR = exp(β):

  • OR > 1 → the predictor increases stay-home odds (positive β)
  • OR < 1 → the predictor decreases stay-home odds (negative β)
  • OR = 1 → no effect (β = 0)

Because log-odds are unbounded, a coefficient of zero corresponds to 50/50 odds (P = 0.5). Positive log-odds means the agent is more likely to stay home than go out; negative means the opposite.

3. Reading a coefficient table

Every regression table in this paper has the same columns:

  • Coef — the β value, the change in log-odds for a one-unit change in that predictor (other predictors held constant)
  • SE — the standard error of β
  • OR — exp(Coef), the multiplicative effect on odds
  • 95% CI — the 95% confidence interval, β ± 1.96·SE
  • Sig — significance markers (*** p < 0.001, ** p < 0.01, * p < 0.05)

One model’s table is shown interactively in Figure 23 (Cohort Analysis); per-model tables for all 21 LLM configurations are in Appendix B.

4. Fixed-effects vs. random-effects

The 100 agents are not interchangeable: each has its own personality, age, and gender. There are two ways the regression can handle that:

  • Fixed-effects logit: include a dummy variable for each agent (factor(agent_id)). The 99 dummies absorb every difference between agents, so only the infection coefficients are reported. Useful when the question is just “what is the shape of the infection curve, controlling for who the agent is?”
  • Random-effects logit: instead of 99 dummies, treat each agent’s baseline as a draw from a Normal distribution (a random intercept). This is more efficient and lets trait, gender, and age stay in the model as predictors instead of being absorbed by the dummies.

Both specifications are reported side-by-side in Figure 23. Diagnostic validation for both is in Appendix C1 (fixed-effects) and C2 (random-effects).

D.2 — Understanding Spearman’s Rank Correlation (ρ)

1. What is Spearman’s ρ?

Spearman’s ρ is a non-parametric measure of how well two rankings agree. It cares only about ordering, not absolute values, so it works equally well for raw rates, log-transformed rates, or anything else that can be sorted.

  • ρ = +1: perfect agreement (identical rankings)
  • ρ = 0: no relationship (random orderings)
  • ρ = −1: reversed rankings

2. How it’s computed

Rank each variable from lowest (1) to highest (n); for each item, compute d = (rank in A) − (rank in B); then:

ρ = 1 − (6 × Σd²) / (n × (n² − 1))

If two items tie, they share the average of their tied ranks (e.g., a tie for 19th and 20th gives both a rank of 19.5).
