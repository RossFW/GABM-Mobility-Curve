Author Note — Statistical Methods
Understanding Spearman’s Rank Correlation (ρ)

1. What is Spearman’s rank correlation?

A non-parametric measure of how well two variables’ rankings agree. Unlike Pearson correlation, it doesn’t assume linearity — it only cares about ordering. If agents who rank high in one model also rank high in another, ρ is close to +1.

  • ρ = +1: perfect agreement (identical rankings)
  • ρ = 0: no relationship (random orderings)
  • ρ = −1: perfect disagreement (reversed rankings)

2. Worked example with 5 agents

Consider 5 agents simulated by two different LLM configs. Each agent’s stay-home rate is the proportion of decisions where they chose to stay home.

AgentConfig A rateRank AConfig B rateRank Bdd²
Alice0.8550.72411
Bob0.3010.452−11
Carol0.6030.55300
Dave0.4520.38111
Eve0.7040.805−11

Step 1: Rank each column from lowest (1) to highest (5).

Step 2: Compute d = Rank A − Rank B for each agent.

Step 3: Apply the formula:

ρ = 1 − (6 × Σd²) / (n × (n² − 1))

ρ = 1 − (6 × 4) / (5 × 24) = 1 − 24/120 = 1 − 0.2 = 0.80

Interpretation: ρ = 0.80 indicates strong agreement — the two models largely agree on which agents are cautious and which are bold.

3. How we use it in Figure 29

  • Instead of 5 agents, we have 100
  • Instead of 2 configs, we compute ρ for all 21 × 21 = 441 pairs (210 unique off-diagonal pairs)
  • Each agent’s stay-home rate is computed across all 200 decisions (40 infection levels × 5 reps)
  • High ρ across model pairs → agents are “real individuals” whose traits drive behavior consistently
  • Low ρ → the LLM model matters more than the agent’s traits

4. Interpreting the heatmap

  • Dark cells = high ρ (strong agreement on agent ordering)
  • Light cells = low ρ (models rank agents differently)
  • The diagonal is always 1.0 (a config correlated with itself)
  • Look for provider blocks: do models from the same provider agree more with each other than with models from other providers?
Author Note — Key Finding
The Three Forces: Intercept, Personality, and Infection

The decision equation

Each agent’s probability of staying home is governed by three forces combined in the logit:

P(stay home) = logistic(intercept + log(personality OR) + log(infection OR))

The agent flips from “go out” to “stay home” (P > 50%) when the sum crosses zero. Each force plays a distinct role:

  • Intercept = baseline gravity. A negative intercept pulls all agents toward going out; a positive one pulls toward staying home. Same for everyone.
  • Personality OR = the agent-specific push. Varies by agent based on their trait combination. A conscientious, agreeable agent might have combined OR = 10,000; an extraverted, emotionally stable agent might have OR = 0.00001.
  • Infection OR = the situational push. Grows with infection level, same for all agents. At 0% infection, OR = 1 (no effect). At 7%, it can reach 10² to 10&sup4; depending on the model.

Why relative magnitude matters (Fig 27)

Figure 27 plots personality ORs and infection ORs on the same log scale. This reveals a fundamental difference in how LLMs weight situation vs. character:

ModelPersonality OR rangeInfection OR at 7%InterceptImplication
Claude Sonnet 4.510&sup5; spread~10³−7.5Personality-driven: some agents’ traits can never be overcome by infection
GPT-5.2 (low)~2 orders~10³−0.6Infection-driven: infection overwhelms all personalities by ~1%
GPT-4o~5 orders~10³−9.2Intercept-anchored: strong negative baseline keeps mobility high despite infection exceeding personality

The GPT-4o puzzle: why infection OR > personality isn’t enough

GPT-4o’s infection curve exceeds its personality cloud in Fig 33, yet its mobility curve (Fig 9, yellow line) stays relatively high. The intercept explains this: at −9.2, the model has a strong baseline pull toward going out. For an agent to flip to staying home, the combined personality + infection push must exceed 9.2 in log-odds space — a high bar.

Compare GPT-5.2 (low) with intercept −0.6: agents are nearly indifferent at baseline. The slightest infection push flips them. This is why its mobility curve collapses immediately.

What this means for the mobility curve shape

The shape of a model’s mobility curve is the visible consequence of how it balances these three forces:

  • Wide personality spread + moderate intercept → gradual S-curve. Agents peel off one by one as infection overwhelms each agent’s unique threshold. (Claude Sonnet 4.5)
  • Narrow personality spread + near-zero intercept → cliff. All agents flip together because they have similar thresholds. (GPT-5.2 low)
  • Any personality spread + very negative intercept → high-riding curve that resists dropping. The “gravity” toward going out is so strong that even large infection ORs struggle to flip the decision. (GPT-4o, Claude Opus 4.5)

The deeper question

This reveals that LLMs differ not just in how sensitive they are to infection, but in what they prioritize. Some models treat the agent’s personality as the dominant factor in decision-making; others treat the situational case count as dominant. This is a model-level characteristic — a reflection of how each LLM internally weights character vs. circumstance when simulating human behavior.
